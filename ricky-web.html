Distinguishing Real Planets from Fake Ones

Professor Dusan S. Danilovic

Advanced research in exoplanet detection techniques, integrating space-based missions with ground-based surveys to enhance detection accuracy and minimize false positives in crowded stellar fields.

Project Focus

Researched and applied advanced detection techniques to identify and analyze exoplanets, with a focus on distinguishing real exoplanets from false positives.

Key Contributions

  • • Explored various exoplanet detection methods, including the transit method and radial velocity method, analyzing their strengths and weaknesses.
  • • Integrated data from space-based missions (TESS) with ground-based surveys (ATLAS and ZTF) to enhance detection accuracy and spatial resolution.
  • • Utilized aperture photometry and least-squares fit to a two-dimensional elliptical Gaussian profile for precise measurement of stellar brightness.
  • • Developed methodologies to minimize false positives, particularly in crowded stellar fields, by carefully analyzing photometric measurements.

Exploring Quarkonium

Professor Daniel Marlow

Investigating the theoretical and computational properties of quarkonium systems (bound states of heavy quark–antiquark pairs) within the framework of quantum mechanics and quantum chromodynamics (QCD).

Project Focus

Investigating the theoretical and computational properties of quarkonium systems within the framework of quantum mechanics and quantum chromodynamics.

Key Contributions

  • • Studying the Schrödinger equation with effective potentials (such as the Cornell potential) to model quark confinement and strong interaction behavior.
  • • Developing numerical simulations (shooting method and finite difference methods) to solve radial wavefunctions and energy eigenvalues for quarkonium states.
  • • Analyzing relativistic corrections and spin-dependent interactions to better approximate experimental spectra.
  • • Collaborating on computational optimization techniques to address numerical instability and overflow issues in high-energy physics simulations.
  • • Connecting theoretical results with experimental observations from particle physics to deepen understanding of strong force dynamics.

Wildlife Protection and Ecological Development Pilot Project

Southeast Tibet • Chayu County

🦁

Project Overview

A comprehensive ecological conservation initiative centered in Chayu County, Tibet, addressing the critical link between wildlife protection and sustainable human-nature coexistence. The project integrates scientific monitoring, policy frameworks, and community engagement to protect rare species while supporting local livelihoods.

Key Contributions & Insights

  • Scientific Monitoring: Analyzed infrared camera data confirming Chayu County as a vital biodiversity area, documenting stable populations of apex predators and rare ungulates including Clouded Leopard, Golden Cat, Dhole, and Black Musk Deer.
  • Policy Integration: Studied the implementation of governance frameworks such as the "River Chief, Lake Chief, and Forest Chief" systems and judicial measures like special protection orders for endangered species.
  • Human-Wildlife Conflict Resolution: Investigated the paradox of conservation success leading to increased human-wildlife conflict, particularly regarding bear and macaque damage to crops.
  • Technological Innovation: Explored AI-powered, real-time warning systems as bridges between ecological protection and community safety assurance.
  • Sustainable Development: Developed understanding of achieving "Ecological Beauty, Industrial Prosperity, and People's Wealth" through integrating species monitoring, conflict analysis, and policy recommendations.
  • Cultural Wisdom: Recognized the profound ecological value embedded in traditional beliefs and cultural practices, viewing them as time-tested rules for maintaining ecological balance.

Core Philosophy

Effective ecological protection is not a singular scientific endeavor but a systemic undertaking requiring integration of scientific data, policy, economic considerations, and cultural wisdom. Continuity is achieved when conservation is internalized by the local community and becomes a sustainable way of life, ensuring the community becomes a proactive partner rather than a passive recipient in the conservation process.

Impact & Vision

The Chayu pilot project demonstrates that protection is achieved by integrating science, technology, and policy to safeguard ecosystems, while wisdom of continuity is realized by weaving conservation into the fabric of community life. This holistic approach ensures that wildlife and the unique ecological system of Southeast Tibet will be truly protected and sustained for generations to come.
